Xiamen’s Hidden Gems Revealed by English-Speaking Locals

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

When most travelers think of Xiamen, their minds immediately drift to the iconic Gulangyu Island, with its car-free streets and colonial architecture, or the bustling Zhongshan Road Pedestrian Street. These spots are undeniably beautiful, but they often overshadow the deeper, more authentic experiences that make this coastal city in Fujian Province so special. To truly understand Xiamen, you need to go beyond the guidebooks and explore the places where locals spend their time—the hidden corners where tradition, nature, and modern life intersect. And who better to guide you than English-speaking residents who call this city home? They bridge the gap between visitor and local, offering insights you won’t find anywhere else.

Beyond Gulangyu: The Local’s Xiamen

While Gulangyu is a must-see, it’s often crowded with tourists. English-speaking locals love the island but know how to experience it differently. Instead of just visiting the Sunlight Rock or the piano museums, they recommend exploring the quieter, southern part of the island early in the morning. Here, you’ll find narrow pathways leading to secluded beaches and hidden gardens where you can hear the sound of pianos drifting from open windows—a reminder of the island’s musical heritage—without the crowds. Another insider tip is to visit the lesser-known churches and villas, like the Huang Family Garden, which tells a story of a bygone era without the hustle.

But the real magic lies beyond Gulangyu. Xiamen is a city of contrasts, where ancient temples sit beside trendy cafes, and fishing villages retain their charm despite urban development. It’s this blend that makes the city so captivating.

Shapowei: Where Art Meets Tradition

Tucked away near Xiamen University, Shapowei is a former fishing port that has transformed into a hub for artists, designers, and coffee lovers. English-speaking locals often gather here for its bohemian vibe and creative energy. The area is characterized by colorful murals, independent galleries, and quirky boutiques selling handmade crafts. One of the highlights is the Cat Street—a narrow alley filled with feline-themed art and cafes where you can enjoy a cup of locally roasted coffee while watching artists at work.

But Shapowei isn’t just about aesthetics; it’s deeply rooted in history. The old fishing docks still exist, and you can see traditional wooden boats bobbing in the water as modern youth discuss art and philosophy in nearby cafes. It’s a perfect example of how Xiamen honors its past while embracing the future. Locals recommend visiting in the late afternoon when the light casts a golden glow over the area, making it ideal for photography or simply soaking in the atmosphere.

Yundang Lake: A Urban Oasis

Many tourists overlook Yundang Lake, but for locals, it’s a beloved spot for relaxation and exercise. Once a polluted waterway, it has been transformed into a beautiful urban lake surrounded by parks, walking paths, and modern sculptures. English-speaking residents often come here for a morning jog or an evening stroll, enjoying the views of the city skyline reflected in the water. The lake is particularly enchanting at night when the lights from surrounding buildings create a shimmering effect.

Along the lake, you’ll find cafes and tea houses where locals gather to chat and unwind. It’s a great place to practice your English with friendly residents who are always happy to share stories about Xiamen’s development and culture. Don’t miss the chance to try some local snacks from vendors near the lake, such as peanut soup or oyster omelets—a delicious way to experience the city’s culinary delights.

Culinary Secrets: From Street Food to Fine Dining

Xiamen’s food scene is a highlight for any visitor, but most stick to popular spots like the night markets on Zhongshan Road. Locals, however, know where to find the most authentic flavors. One hidden gem is the Xiaoping Road area, where small family-run restaurants serve traditional Min Nan cuisine. Dishes like tusundong (a savory pork rib soup) and shachamian (a noodle dish with a rich, peanut-based sauce) are must-tries. English-speaking foodies often recommend these spots for their authenticity and warmth.

Another insider favorite is the Jimei District, just outside the city center. Here, you’ll find bustling markets offering fresh seafood straight from the Taiwan Strait. Locals often take visitors to these markets to pick out fish, shrimp, and crabs, which are then cooked on the spot at nearby restaurants. It’s an immersive experience that combines food, culture, and interaction with the community.

For those with a sweet tooth, Xiamen has plenty to offer. Mahua, a twisted dough pastry, and putian sweet rice balls are local desserts that you won’t find in many tourist guides. English-speaking locals might direct you to a small shop in the Siming District where these treats have been made for generations.

Hidden Temples and Spiritual Retreats

While Nanputuo Temple is famous for its grandeur and history, locals prefer quieter spiritual sites for contemplation. One such place is the Hongshan Park, home to a lesser-known temple dedicated to the goddess Mazu. Perched on a hill, it offers panoramic views of the city and the sea. English-speaking residents often visit here for meditation or to enjoy the peaceful gardens away from the crowds.

Another hidden gem is the Qingjiao Ciji Temple on the outskirts of Xiamen. This temple complex is surrounded by lush forests and hiking trails, making it a perfect destination for nature lovers. Locals appreciate its serene atmosphere and the opportunity to connect with nature while exploring Buddhist culture. It’s not uncommon to see monks chanting or residents practicing tai chi in the courtyard—a glimpse into the spiritual side of Xiamen that many tourists miss.

Coastal Escapes: Beaches and Islands Beyond the Crowds

Xiamen’s coastline is dotted with beautiful beaches, but most visitors only know about Baicheng Beach near Hulishan Fortress. Locals, however, prefer the quieter shores of Huangcuo Beach or the nearby island of Xiang’an. Here, you can enjoy pristine sands and clear waters without the crowds. English-speaking residents often organize beach picnics or bonfires here, especially during the summer months.

For a more adventurous experience, locals recommend taking a boat to the smaller islands like Dadeng Island, where you can explore traditional fishing villages and sample fresh seafood. The island is also known for its role in historical conflicts, adding a layer of depth to your visit. English-speaking guides—often locals themselves—can provide fascinating insights into the area’s history and culture.

Modern Xiamen: Art Districts and Innovation Hubs

Xiamen isn’t just about history and nature; it’s also a city of innovation and creativity. The Xinglin Bay Business District, for example, is home to modern architecture, tech startups, and cultural centers. English-speaking professionals often frequent this area for its trendy cafes and networking events. It’s a great place to see how Xiamen is evolving into a global city while maintaining its unique identity.

Another modern hidden gem is the Xiamen Red Design Center, a revitalized industrial area now housing art installations, design studios, and pop-up markets. Locals love this space for its dynamic energy and the way it blends old and new. It’s not uncommon to find exhibitions here that showcase local artists alongside international talent, making it a must-visit for culture enthusiasts.

Practical Tips from Locals

To make the most of your trip, English-speaking locals emphasize the importance of timing. Visit popular spots like Gulangyu early in the morning or late in the afternoon to avoid crowds. Use public transportation, such as the BRT (Bus Rapid Transit) or the metro, to navigate the city like a resident. Xiamen is also very bike-friendly, with numerous sharing platforms available for exploring at your own pace.

When it comes to communication, don’t worry if you don’t speak Mandarin or the local Min Nan dialect. Many young residents in Xiamen are fluent in English and eager to help. Apps like WeChat and Google Translate can also bridge any language gaps, but the best way to connect is through a smile and a willingness to engage.

Finally, locals advise taking time to simply wander. Xiamen’s charm often reveals itself in unexpected moments—a conversation with a street vendor, the smell of jasmine tea from a traditional chaguan (tea house), or the sight of sunset over the Taiwan Strait. These are the experiences that will stay with you long after you’ve left.
